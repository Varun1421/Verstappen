Cross-Sport Greats

Relative Dominance Across Sports

Directly comparing an F1 race finish to a tennis tournament result is misleading. Instead, each event is converted into a sport-relative dominance percentile from 0 to 1, where 1 means the best performer in that field. The curve then asks: how often did this athlete perform near the top of their own sport?

Common scale Each result becomes a percentile within its own competitive field, not a raw cross-sport rank.
Curve height The y-value answers: what share of events cleared that dominance threshold. When the curve steps down, fewer events met the stricter cutoff.
Curve shape A curve that stays high farther to the right means the athlete was repeatedly near the very top of their field.
Focus athletes

Dominance percentile is computed as 1 − ((finish rank − 1) ÷ (field size − 1)). Field sizes are sport-specific approximations: 20-car F1 field, 128-player tennis draw, 22-player soccer match, 11-player cricket batting side and 8-lane Olympic final. Soccer and cricket match-impact ranks are approximated from each athlete's scoring, role and award-dominance season. The gray band is a typical elite benchmark, not an average athlete.

Data & Methodology

On Measuring Dominance

This project compares dominance in two ways: first inside Formula 1, where the data is race-by-race and season-based, and then across sports, where raw totals have to be translated into a common scale before they can be interpreted responsibly.

The Formula 1 portion uses local CSV tables for the 2023 season: race-by-race championship points, Verstappen and Pérez's Red Bull comparison metrics, and a manually assembled table of historically dominant F1 seasons. These files drive the animated standings, teammate comparison, small multiples, and parallel-coordinates chart.

Historical F1 comparisons were normalized before plotting. Win rate, podium rate, pole rate and fastest-lap rate are computed as counts divided by races entered. Points capture is computed as average points per race divided by the maximum points available per race in that scoring era. Reliability is represented as one minus the share of races that ended in a DNF.

Within F1

The project compares drivers and seasons using rates instead of raw totals so shorter seasons and older scoring systems remain comparable.

Across Sports

Each event is converted into a sport-relative percentile from 0 to 1, where 1 means best performer in that competitive field.

Interpretation

The goal is not to declare a single greatest season, but to compare how sustained separation from peers shows up in different sports.

The cross-sport ECDF uses a separate CSV in the data folder. For each selected season, performances are expressed as dominance percentiles: 1 − ((rank − 1) ÷ (field size − 1)). A higher value means the athlete finished closer to the top of their own event field, not that one sport is inherently harder than another.

The y-value on that chart answers a simple question: what share of this athlete's events reached or exceeded a given dominance threshold? When the curve steps down, the athlete did not get worse over time. The x-axis threshold simply became stricter. For example, Verstappen can clear broad "near the top" thresholds in nearly every race, then drop once the threshold requires event-best or near-perfect results.

Comparing Verstappen to Federer, Messi, Kohli or Phelps directly would be misleading. This project instead compares each athlete against their own field first, then compares those sport-relative dominance patterns.

Limitations

  • Cross-sport comparisons are exploratory, not definitive rankings.
  • Era, equipment, calendar length and team context still matter.
  • Soccer and cricket percentiles use approximated match-impact rankings.
  • Final calculations and chart interpretations were reviewed manually.
